The Science of Sustained Attention

By M. Okafor · 14 min read · Cognition

Attention is not a single faculty but a negotiation between competing systems: one that seeks novelty, and one that sustains effort. Most knowledge work asks us to hold the second system steady while the first is bombarded with the most sophisticated distraction machinery ever built.

The research is unambiguous about the cost. After an interruption, returning to the original task takes far longer than the interruption itself — the mind must rebuild the entire context it dropped. For people whose attention systems already run hot, that rebuilding tax compounds across the day.

The problem is rarely a lack of intention. It is that intention decays faster than the environment changes.

Externalizing memory

One consistent finding: offloading a thought the instant it occurs — before it can compete for working memory — preserves both the thought and the task at hand. The tools that work are the ones with the lowest capture cost, measured in keystrokes and seconds.

A capture mechanism that takes ten seconds will be abandoned. One that takes two will be used hundreds of times, and each use is a small transaction: the brain trades an open loop for the confidence that the loop is stored somewhere trustworthy.

Progress you can see

The second lever is visible progress. Streaks, position markers, and resumable state all exploit the same mechanism — they convert an abstract intention into a concrete, partially-completed object the mind wants to finish.

Half-read articles are the canonical failure case. Without a stored position, the cost of resuming rounds up to the cost of starting over, and starting over loses to whatever is newer. With one, resuming becomes the cheapest available action.

Design for the brain you have, not the brain you wish you had. The systems that survive are the ones that assume distraction will happen and make recovery nearly free.
